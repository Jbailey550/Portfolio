Growing a diverse, inclusive design team

Taking NICE's design function from two reactive UX designers to a strategically positioned, multidisciplinary team — built on inclusion, evidence, and organisational trust.

Impact summary
Grew the team from 2 to 7 — adding user research, service design, and digital performance
Half the team from underrepresented groups in the design industry
Shifted the team from tactical execution to strategic discovery and problem-solving
Low turnover, recent promotions, and a team well-respected across the organisation
Project Cover Image

The Challenge

When I joined NICE in 2016, the design team was just two of us. Our UCD maturity was low, but the bigger issue was how we were positioned: operating almost entirely tactically. Requirements were handed down and we were expected to execute them without any room for discovery or validation.

Without a dedicated research function or shared standards, we were stuck in a reactive cycle — delivering features but never having a say in the 'why' behind the work. We were building things, but didn't have the evidence to know if we were solving the right problems for our users.

My Role

My ambition was never just to build a big team, but to build one that was diverse and effective — one that could genuinely make things better for our users and the organisation. I wanted to bring in the right roles, set a standard of excellence, and most importantly, ensure the team was positioned correctly so they could do their jobs to the best of their ability.

The Approach

Establishing foundations. I started by creating the frameworks we'd been missing — ensuring that when new people joined, they weren't walking into chaos, but into a professional environment with clear UCD standards. This included a discovery playbook, design critique structure, decision logs, a design system, and a new starters guide.

Intentional growth. I advocated for team expansion to fill critical skill gaps, beginning with User Research. I consistently highlighted the link between specialised roles and high-quality outputs, demonstrating how aligning with DDaT best practices would amplify our impact and result in better outcomes for the organisation and users.

"My ambition was never just to build a big team, but to build one which was diverse and effective and could genuinely make things better for our users."

Inclusive recruitment. I was intentional about building diversity and inclusion into the team from day one. I diversified our recruitment channels and rewrote job descriptions to remove exclusionary barriers — such as unnecessary degree requirements or inflated experience levels. I modernised our interview process by providing candidates with questions 24 hours in advance, moving away from putting people on the spot and giving candidates space for thoughtful, evidence-based answers.

Strategic positioning. I worked to move myself and the team out of the "tactical" bucket. By joining leadership meetings and engaging with stakeholders early, I shifted our role from just "making things look good" to strategic problem-solving with a seat at the table.

Outcome

  • Moved from a team of 2 senior UX designers to a multidisciplinary team of 7 — including 2 user researchers, 1 service designer, 2 UX designers, and 2 digital performance specialists
  • Built a diverse team with half the team coming from underrepresented groups in the design industry
  • A thriving team with recent promotions and low turnover — well-respected for the value they bring across the organisation
  • Shifted to a more proactive working model, with discovery-led practice increasing UCD maturity and delivering better outcomes for users
