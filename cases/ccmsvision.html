Rethinking content management for the next generation of digital publishing

A ground-up vision project for CCMS — defining the future state of a platform used by editorial teams across a global media network, from stakeholder alignment through to high-fidelity prototypes.

Cover image — replace with img tag

The Challenge

Editorial teams across the network were working around their own tools. CCMS, the organisation's central content management system, had been built incrementally over a decade — each iteration solving a specific pain point but never addressing the underlying architecture. The result was a platform that felt foreign to the people who relied on it most.

Research indicated that journalists were spending upwards of 30% of their working day navigating the system rather than creating. Version conflicts, opaque publishing workflows, and a lack of real-time collaboration meant that high-pressure deadlines were being met through workarounds, spreadsheets, and institutional knowledge rather than the platform itself.

The ask was ambitious: define a vision for what CCMS could become — not just an incremental improvement, but a genuine step-change in how editorial teams experience their core tools.

My Role

I led the vision design process from initial scoping through to a tested, high-fidelity prototype. Working directly with product leadership, I structured a discovery programme that gave us the evidence base needed to make bold design decisions — and to bring stakeholders along on the journey.

This wasn't a traditional project brief with a defined output. It required building trust with editorial leadership, translating ambiguous feedback into clear design principles, and navigating genuine tension between what users wanted and what the platform could realistically support.

The Approach

The project opened with a structured discovery phase: 14 stakeholder interviews across editorial, product, and engineering, supplemented by contextual observation sessions with journalists during live publishing cycles. This gave us both the strategic context and the raw, ground-level insight needed to design with confidence.

I developed three distinct concept directions — each grounded in the same evidence base but pulling on different strategic bets about how editorial workflows would evolve. Rather than presenting polished concepts, we brought rough directional prototypes to co-design workshops. The goal was to surface disagreement early, not avoid it.

"The hardest part wasn't designing the future — it was creating the shared language that let us all talk about it in the same way."

From that synthesis, a single direction emerged — one that prioritised real-time collaboration, contextual guidance, and a dramatically simplified publishing workflow. I moved into high-fidelity design with a clear set of principles to anchor every decision.

Outcome

The vision prototype was presented to executive stakeholders and adopted as the strategic roadmap for CCMS development over the following two-year cycle. More significantly, the process itself shifted how the product team approached user research — moving from reactive problem-solving to structured, evidence-led design strategy.

  • Unified product vision — for the first time, editorial, product, and engineering teams had a shared, visual reference point for where the platform was heading
  • Validated through testing — the prototype was tested with 8 editorial teams across 4 markets, surfacing critical nuances in workflow that shaped the final design direction
  • Reduced decision latency — by establishing clear design principles early, the team cut design review cycles from weeks to days during subsequent delivery phases
  • Became the brief — rather than being filed away, the vision prototype was used directly by engineering leadership to scope the first delivery increment

Reflection

Vision work without a clear delivery horizon is a double-edged brief. The freedom to think expansively is valuable, but without the discipline of constraints, it's easy to produce something inspiring that never lands anywhere. What kept this grounded was committing to testable prototypes early — even rough ones — rather than polished decks that could mean anything to anyone.

The thing I'd do differently: I'd involve engineering partners much earlier in the concept stage. Not to sanity-check feasibility, but because their perspective on system complexity often surfaces the most interesting design constraints — and in this case, those constraints pointed toward genuinely better solutions than we'd arrived at on our own.
